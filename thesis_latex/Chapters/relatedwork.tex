This chapter reviews prior work that grounds the four contributions of the
thesis. It is organised into five strands. Section~\ref{sec:rw_compression}
covers context compression and agentic memory; Section~\ref{sec:rw_multiagent}
covers multi-agent systems and the closest published industry analogue;
Section~\ref{sec:rw_rag} covers retrieval-augmented generation, long-context
benchmarks, and the evaluation suite that frames C3;
Section~\ref{sec:rw_privacy} covers privacy-aware retrieval and adversarial
robustness; and Section~\ref{sec:rw_fcg} situates the work within the Future
Computing Group programme at the University of Oulu and the closely related
TalentAdore industry use case.

\section{Context Compression and Agentic Memory}
\label{sec:rw_compression}

\subsection{Hard-prompt token filtering}

The first generation of practical context-compression techniques operated
at the token level. Jiang \emph{et al.}~\cite{jiang2023llmlingua} introduced
LLMLingua, which uses a small autoregressive language model to score
tokens by their per-step perplexity contribution and drop those whose
removal least disturbs the conditional distribution. The same line of work
produced LongLLMLingua~\cite{jiang2024longllmlingua}, which extends the
coarse-to-fine ranking with a question-aware step to handle the retrieval
setting, and LLMLingua-2~\cite{pan2024llmlingua2}, which trains an
XLM-RoBERTa token classifier on data distilled from a more capable model,
yielding a task-agnostic compressor that runs natively on CPU and on
Apple's MPS backend.

This thesis takes LLMLingua-2 as the principal hard-prompt baseline.
Pan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{pan2024llmlingua2} report substantial token
reductions at modest accuracy costs on long-context QA, and the
model is bundled with the official \texttt{llmlingua} package, which
makes it the de facto baseline against which any new compressor must be
calibrated. The thesis includes a Month-1 stop-the-line criterion that
NarrativeQA F1 must reproduce within $2$ pp and HotpotQA F1 within $3$ pp
of the reported numbers~\cite{kocisky2018narrativeqa, yang2018hotpotqa}.

\subsection{Soft-prompt compression}

A parallel line of work compresses passages into a small number of
continuous embedding slots that occupy the same positions in a frozen
decoder's attention windows. Mu~\emph{et al.}~\cite{mu2023gist}
introduced \emph{gist tokens}, special positions trained with a masked
attention pattern to summarise a prefix prompt. Chevalier~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{chevalier2023autocompressor} extended this idea with
\emph{AutoCompressor}, which adapts a frozen decoder via LoRA-trained
\emph{summary vectors} and uses a token-level reconstruction loss as the
training objective; their result that a small LoRA adapter is sufficient
on the encoder side, with the decoder kept frozen, is the design
principle this thesis inherits.

Ge~\emph{et al.}~\cite{ge2024icae} formalised this architecture as the
\emph{In-context Autoencoder (ICAE)}, training the encoder to emit a
fixed number of memory tokens that, when re-injected into the decoder,
allow it to reconstruct the source passage. Wang~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{wang2024icf} present the In-Context Former, a faster cross-
attention compressor that drops the reconstruction objective entirely.
Fei~\emph{et al.}~\cite{fei2024semantic} compress at the semantic-unit
level rather than the token level. Rae~\emph{et al.}~\cite{rae2020compressive}
establish that compressed memory in transformers has a degradation curve
in the first place, but report smooth monotonic decay in the
single-context regime --- a baseline that H2 explicitly tests against.

The trainable arm of this thesis is an ICAE-style compressor with the
dual-objective loss documented in Section~\ref{sec:training} of
Chapter~\charef{implementation}. The construction follows Ge~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{ge2024icae} for the architecture and
Chevalier~\emph{et al.}~\cite{chevalier2023autocompressor} for the
reconstruction-loss formulation. The contrastive term is an InfoNCE
loss~\cite{oord2018cpc, chen2020simclr} sampled over
positive--negative source-fragment pairs.

\subsection{Compression for RAG}

A third strand of work pushes compression into the retrieval-augmented
generation pipeline. Cheng~\emph{et al.}~\cite{cheng2024xrag} propose
\emph{xRAG}, which compresses retrieved chunks to a single soft-prompt
token, anchoring the extreme end of the ratio spectrum.
Guo~\emph{et al.}~\cite{guo2025dynamic} make compression
\emph{relevance-conditional}: chunks whose retrieval scores exceed a
threshold are kept verbatim, those below are compressed, and those well
below are discarded. The third pipeline of this thesis (P3) follows
their structural design directly, while the first two pipelines (P1 and
P2) reproduce the classical \emph{compress--then--retrieve} and
\emph{retrieve--then--compress} configurations against which P3 must
demonstrate a benefit. Li~\emph{et al.}~\cite{li2025promptsurvey}
publish a survey that consolidates this design space and was used as a
checklist of comparators during the C3 design.

\subsection{LLM-as-operating-system memory hierarchies}

Packer~\emph{et al.}~\cite{packer2023memgpt} introduced \emph{MemGPT},
which models the LLM as an operating system that pages information between
a fast in-context working set and a slow archive. Xu~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{xu2025amem} propose \emph{A-MEM}, which extends this with
Zettelkasten-style linking. Chhikara~\emph{et al.}~\cite{chhikara2025mem0}
and Rasmussen~\emph{et al.}~\cite{rasmussen2025zep} target the production
end of the spectrum with embedding-based extraction (\emph{Mem0}) and
temporal knowledge-graph memory (\emph{Zep}). Yu~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{yu2025memagent} learn to retrieve from past actions via
reinforcement learning. The memory bus in this thesis is not a
replacement for any of these but a substrate on top of which they could
sit: the storage layer protocols are factored so that an OS-style pager,
a knowledge graph, or an episodic store all conform to the same
contract.

\section{Multi-Agent Systems and Coordination}
\label{sec:rw_multiagent}

\subsection{Multi-agent frameworks}

Wu~\emph{et al.}~\cite{wu2023autogen} introduced \emph{AutoGen}, a
conversational multi-agent framework with role-based agents and tool
use that became the de facto research vehicle for studying
planner-worker-critic topologies. Hong~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{hong2024metagpt} introduced \emph{MetaGPT}, which encodes
standardised operating procedures (SOPs) as structured artefacts passed
between agents. Li~\emph{et al.}~\cite{li2023camel} explored
role-playing dialogues for autonomous agents (\emph{CAMEL}).
Shinn~\emph{et al.}~\cite{shinn2023reflexion} introduced
\emph{Reflexion}, which formalises the critic loop as verbal
reinforcement learning. This thesis adopts AutoGen v0.4 as the agent
runtime; the planner-worker-critic loop is a
\texttt{RoundRobinGroupChat} over a planner, a configurable number of
workers, and a critic that emits a structured \texttt{DONE} or
\texttt{REVISE} verdict in the spirit of Reflexion.

\subsection{Token-efficient multi-agent coordination}

A small but growing literature directly attacks the multi-agent
token-efficiency problem. Zhang~\emph{et al.}~\cite{zhang2024agentprune}
sparsify the communication graph between agents. Wang~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{wang2025agentdropout} drop agents stochastically during
training. Ye~\emph{et al.}~\cite{ye2025kvcomm} share KV-cache prefixes
across agents to amortise communication cost.
Park~\emph{et al.}~\cite{park2025collaborative} introduce
\emph{Collaborative Memory}, which pushes per-fragment access-control
metadata into a shared memory layer for multi-user agent systems; this
is the closest published precedent for the C4 tag-preserving
extension and the structural ancestor of the per-slot tag head in
Section~\ref{sec:c4_design} of Chapter~\charef{implementation}.

\subsection{The closest published industry analogue}

Anthropic's published descriptions of their multi-agent research system
provide the closest industry analogue to the empirical question of this
thesis. Their engineering blog~\cite{anthropic2025multiagent} reports
that \emph{token usage} alone explains approximately eighty percent of
the performance variance in a multi-agent research workflow, and their
context-engineering write-up~\cite{anthropic2025contextengineering}
names four primitives --- \emph{compaction}, \emph{tool-result clearing},
\emph{memory}, and \emph{just-in-time retrieval} --- that frame the
design choices in C3 (RAG pipeline placement). Their announcement of
context editing and the memory tool on the Claude Developer
Platform~\cite{anthropic2025contextediting} reports an
\emph{eighty-four percent token reduction on a 100-turn web-search
evaluation} via context editing alone. These results are the strongest
signal in the public literature that something like a coordination cliff
exists; H2 in this thesis is the first attempt the author is aware of to
characterise its shape empirically.

\subsection{Context engineering for multi-agent assistants}

Haseeb~\cite{haseeb2025contextengineering} attacks the related question
of training-distribution mismatch in multi-agent code assistants; the H3
training-distribution experiment of this thesis is directly motivated by
his finding that compressors trained on monolithic QA underperform when
deployed against dialogue traces. The stretch goal S3
(Chapter~\charef{summary}) would implement his approach as a baseline
comparator on C1.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Long-Context Benchmarks}
\label{sec:rw_rag}

\subsection{Retrieval-augmented generation}

Lewis~\emph{et al.}~\cite{lewis2020rag} introduced the original
retrieval-augmented generation framework, which combines a parametric
generator with a non-parametric retrieval index. The descendant literature
has explored hierarchical
indices~\cite{sarthi2024raptor}, graph
summaries~\cite{edge2024graphrag}, neurobiologically inspired
memory~\cite{gutierrez2024hipporag, gutierrez2025hipporag2}, and
self-reflective gating that decides on the fly whether to
retrieve~\cite{asai2024selfrag}. Anthropic's contextual-retrieval
write-up~\cite{anthropic2024contextualretrieval} reports that prepending
a Claude-generated chunk-level summary to each document chunk before
embedding improves retrieval across codebases, fiction, arXiv preprints,
and scientific papers at a stated cost of approximately
USD~$1.02$ per million document tokens with prompt caching --- a
useful pre-processing comparator for P1 in the C3 catalogue.

The C3 catalogue of this thesis (P1, P2, P3) sits at the intersection of
retrieval and compression. P1 is the \emph{compress-then-retrieve}
configuration, where the index stores compressed chunks; P2 is the
\emph{retrieve-then-compress} configuration that recovers the
LongLLMLingua setup~\cite{jiang2024longllmlingua}; P3 is the
\emph{joint} configuration that routes chunks through one of three
branches based on retrieval relevance, following the structural design of
\cite{guo2025dynamic}.

\subsection{Long-context benchmarks and the \emph{lost-in-the-middle} problem}

Liu~\emph{et al.}~\cite{liu2024lostinmiddle} document the
\emph{lost-in-the-middle} phenomenon: language models attend
disproportionately to the beginning and end of their context windows.
Hsieh~\emph{et al.}~\cite{hsieh2024ruler} introduce the RULER benchmark,
which probes the \emph{effective} context size of long-context LLMs by
constructing tasks at varying needle-in-haystack difficulties.
Bai~\emph{et al.}~\cite{bai2024longbench} maintain LongBench, a suite of
long-context tasks that this thesis uses as an external calibration
anchor. Yen~\emph{et al.}~\cite{yen2025helmet} introduce
\emph{HELMET}, a more recent long-context capability suite that is used
to validate the 70B int4 inference path before any C1 numbers are
collected.

\subsection{Agent-task benchmarks}

GAIA~\cite{mialon2023gaia} and AgentBench~\cite{liu2024agentbench}
motivate the agent-task framing of this thesis. The C1 benchmark is
\emph{not} a competitor to either; it isolates the specific question of
coordination-quality-versus-compression, which neither GAIA nor AgentBench
poses directly. GAIA Level 1 is used as a sanity check that the
planner-worker-critic loop produces non-trivial behaviour at 7B; a
subset of AgentBench environments (OS, DB, LTP) is held in reserve as a
non-critical-path external comparator.

\section{Privacy-Aware Retrieval and Adversarial Robustness}
\label{sec:rw_privacy}

The tag-preserving extension (C4) is grounded in a small but active
literature on privacy-aware retrieval. Zhou~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{zhou2025privacyrag} propose a privacy-aware variant of RAG
that filters retrieved documents against requester-side policy; their
threat model is the structural anchor for the H6 inference-disclosure
metric of this thesis. Bassit and Boddeti~\cite{bassit2025securerag}
push the privacy guarantee deeper, providing cryptographic guarantees on
the retrieval side. Zhao~\cite{zhao2024frag} and Addison~\emph{et
al.}~\cite{addison2024cfedrag} extend RAG to the federated setting, in
which the index itself is distributed across institutional boundaries
that the master's-scale work in this thesis does not cross.

Li~\emph{et al.}~\cite{li2025securitylingua} introduce
\emph{SecurityLingua}, a red-team treatment of prompt compression that
demonstrates adversarial prompts against compressors. The S2 stretch
goal (Chapter~\charef{summary}) would run a SecurityLingua-style
red-team sweep against the tag-preserving variant as part of the
governance-claim hardening.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework~\cite{nistairmf} is cited as the
lattice from which the 5-tier classification hierarchy
(\textsc{public} $<$ \textsc{internal} $<$ \textsc{confidential} $<$
\textsc{restricted} $<$ \textsc{secret}) used by the tag vector
in Section~\ref{sec:datamodel} of
Chapter~\charef{implementation} is derived. This thesis does not claim
NIST compliance; the framework simply provides a vocabulary.

\section{The FCG Programme and the Industry Setting}
\label{sec:rw_fcg}

The thesis sits within the FCG's institutional AI service economy
research programme. Three internal architecture documents specify the
target platform: the system
architecture~\cite{fcgsystemarch2026}, the software
architecture~\cite{fcgsoftwarearch2026}, and the integrator-internal
architecture~\cite{fcgintegratorarch2026}. The financial
analysis~\cite{fcgfinancial2026} sets out a five-year projection for
the University of Oulu that produces a Year-5 benefit-to-cost ratio of
3.5:1 under the Normal scenario, a five-year cumulative net benefit of
approximately EUR~7.7~M, and per-user monthly compute costs that fall
from approximately EUR~20/month (Phase~1, research-active users) to
approximately EUR~11/month (Phase~2). The
use-case document~\cite{fcgusecase2026} narrates a week of an institutional
researcher and is the source of the Vignette~3.7 (Period-end project
reporting) workflow that anchors the C1 benchmark.

Saleh~\emph{et al.}~\cite{saleh2025memindex} introduce \emph{MemIndex},
the FCG group's baseline architecture for distributed agent memory; the
three-layer split (access, compression, storage) used in
Chapter~\charef{implementation} is a direct extension of MemIndex with
compression promoted to a first-class layer. The companion
ACM Computing Surveys paper~\cite{saleh2025messagebrokers} surveys
message brokers for generative AI, evaluating Kafka, NATS, Mosquitto,
RocketMQ, NSQ, VerneMQ, and many others against the GenAI workload, and
proposes the perception--brain--actuation reference architecture for
generative-AI agents that the planner-worker-critic loop of this thesis
instantiates as three concrete actors. The thesis is also informed by
the wider FCG programme on the AI service
economy~\cite{loven2025paper1, loven2025paper2, loven2025paper3,
loven2025paper4}, on the 6G computing
continuum~\cite{loven20256g, kokkonen2022autonomy}, and on
LLM-augmented operations~\cite{sheikhi2025cognitive}.

The industry partner of this thesis, TalentAdore, supplies the
production-pricing constants for the cost model in C3
(GPT-4o-mini and Claude Haiku 4.5) and is the source of the
EUR-per-workflow framing for the cost-arm. Their interest in this thesis
is the cost-vs-coordination trade-off curve, not the C4 governance arm.

\section{Synthesis and Positioning}
\label{sec:rw_synthesis}

In summary, the literature offers strong single-agent compression
techniques (LLMLingua-2 and ICAE), a maturing multi-agent runtime
ecosystem (AutoGen, MetaGPT, CAMEL, Reflexion), a maturing RAG-plus-
compression literature (xRAG, LongLLMLingua, dynamic compression for
RAG), and an emerging privacy-aware retrieval literature
(Privacy-Aware RAG, SecureRAG, FRAG, C-FedRAG). It offers indirect
evidence from industry that coordination cost dominates production
multi-agent deployments (Anthropic's research-system writeup, Anthropic's
context-editing release).
What it does not offer is a systematic empirical
characterisation of how compression degrades multi-agent coordination
across model size --- whether the coordination curve is smooth like
the single-agent QA curve or has a cliff that single-agent metrics
cannot anticipate. The thesis is positioned squarely on that gap, with
the four contributions C1--C4 working together to make the question
empirically tractable on a single workstation.
